\begin{abstract}
Benchmark-based evaluation is the primary method used by the research community to measure
the capabilities of code large language models (LLMs). 
Its reliability depends on a key assumption: that the model has not seen the benchmark during training. However, this assumption is increasingly difficult to guarantee. Widely used benchmarks such as HumanEval and MBPP are publicly available and have been found in the pretraining corpora of many models, causing evaluation scores to reflect memorization rather than genuine generalization. Existing mitigation strategies — corpus filtering, benchmark replacement, or dynamic evaluation — are each insufficient for models that are already trained and deployed. This thesis investigates a novel approach to address this issue: using machine unlearning techniques to remove benchmark-specific knowledge from contaminated code LLMs, thereby restoring the validity of benchmark-based evaluation without the need for costly retraining or new benchmark creation.
However, this assumption is increasingly difficult to guarantee.
Popular benchmarks, such as HumanEval and MBPP, are publicly available and have been found within
the pretraining datasets of many models, leading to evaluation scores that reveal memorization rather than true generalization ability.
Current solution approaches, as corpus filtering, benchmark replacement, or dynamic evaluation, are each insufficient for models that
have already been trained and deployed.
\par\vspace{1em}
This thesis investigates whether a neuron-based machine unlearning approach can be used to
solve the problem of benchmark contamination post-hoc, without requiring retraining.
The main hypothesis is that benchmark memorization may be localized in a subset of neurons
that strongly activate to benchmark samples rather than to structurally equivalent general code.
To test this hypothesis, we design a controlled contamination scenario where a small code LLM is trained on a dataset that intentionally includes a target benchmark, allowing us to simulate a clean contamination scenario where the benchmark is explicitly included in training without any pretraining overlap.
This pipeline identifies those neurons, responsible for benchmark-specific knowledge memorization,
by extracting per-neuron activation patterns using forward hooks, scoring each neuron using
Average Precision scores against a controlled reference dataset, and selecting outliers neurons
using a Z-score-based thresholding procedure.
The selected neurons are then disabled by zeroing the corresponding columns of the MLP down-projection weight matrix,
effectively suppressing their contribution to the model's output --- without requiring any gradient updates, additional data, or retraining.
\par\vspace{1em}
We evaluate the impact of this intervention on both Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B and Qwen3-1.7B, using McEval-Hard
as the contamination target within a controlled experimental scenario.
At a threshold of Z=8, the intervention achieves a significant reduction in benchmark performance,
reducing the model's accuracy from 39.3\% to 32.25\%, matching the performance of a clean baseline that was never exposed to the benchmark,
with a corresponding reduction in structural similarity (TSED score) to canonical solutions.
An additional evaluation analysis on HumanEval+ and MBPP+ shows that, the hypothesis
that masking benchmark-specific neurons do not degrade the general coding capability of the model,
is confirmed.
However, a more detailed task-level anaysis reveals that the intervention neither fully
suppresses memorized tasks nor recovers tasks that were solved by the clean baseline 
and displaced by contamination.
These findings point to a fundamental challenge: the neurons responsible for benchmark
memorization appear to be responsible also for general coding competence.
Overall, these findings confirm neuron-based benchmark unlearning as a promising solution
but yet not sufficient to be claimed as a being complete and reliable, providing an
experimental pipeline and evaluation methodology for future work in this direction.
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}
Large language models for code have become one of the most prominent categories of AI systems, with models achieving high results on code generation benchmarks.
The evaluation of these models is essential: benchmark-based evaluation is the standard approach in the community to compare capabilities, track progress, and decide the model to be deployed in real-world applications.
However, the validity of benchmark-based evaluation is threatened by data contamination: if a model has been trained on the benchmark problems or their solutions, its performance may reflect memorization rather than genuine generalization, leading to inflated scores that do not reflect true coding capability.
If benchmark scores cannot be trusted, the entire evaluation process is compromised, and the community loses a critical tool for measuring progress and comparing models.
\par\vspace{1em}
Many evidences have shown that they cannot be trusted.
Benchmarks such as HumanEval (\citet{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2107-03374}) and MBPP (\citet{austin2021programsynthesislargelanguage}) are publicly available and have been widely distributed across repositories, making it more possible that they were included, in whole or in part, in the pretraining data of many models later evaluated on them.
The main issue is if a model has seen the benchmark during training, the benchmark-based evaluation is no longer a valid measure of the model's coding capability, as it may be simply recalling memorized solutions rather than demonstrating true generalization to unseen problems.
This phenomenon, also called data contamination (or data leakage), has been confirmed by several studies: \citet{riddell2024quantifyingcontaminationevaluatingcode} found out that the performance of a model on contaminated problems is significantly higher than on clean ones.
\citet{matton2024leakagecodegenerationevaluation} found out that Humaneval problems are present at least 43 times in public GitHub repositories.
\citet{zhou2025lessleakbenchinvestigationdataleakage} documented contamination across 83 software engineering benchmarks, with an average contamination ratio of 4.8\%, with even higher rates in widely used benchmarks.
\par\vspace{1em}
The commond strategies to address contamination are: filtering training data before training, or creating new benchmarks.
However, these strategies are not sufficient: filtering is error-prone and incomplete and creating new benchmarks does not solve the problem as they will become contaminated as soon as they are released.
What is needed is a way to remove contamination, focusing on post-hoc solutions that can be applied to existing models without the need for costly retraining or new benchmark creation: a technique that can erase benchmark-specific knowledge from a contaminated model, effectively restoring the validity of benchmark-based evaluation.
\par\vspace{1em}
An encouraging approach is machine unlearning, which refers to the specific removal of some training data or knowledge from a trained model, without retraining from scratch.
Machine unlearning techniques have been developed to address various issues in LLMs, including data privacy, copyright removal, safety alignment, bias mitigation, and hallucination reduction (\citet{liu2024rethinkingmachineunlearninglarge}).
A vast majority of these techniques are gradient-based and operate by modifying the model's parameters to maximize the loss on the data to be forgotten while minimizing the loss on the data to be retained.
Another alternative approach is the neuron-based unlearning: first localize the neurons responsible for retaining target knowledge and then mask those neurons to suppress the retrieval of that knowledge.
This approach has been shown to work effectively well on removing knowledge in multilingual models (\citet{kojima2024multilingualabilitydecoderbasedpretrained}) and sensitive content in code LLMs (\citet{pochinkov2024dissectinglanguagemodelsmachine}).
These promising results suggest that neuron-based unlearning may be similarly effective for removing benchmark-specific knowledge from contaminated code LLMs.

\section{Contributions}
This work investigates the use of neuron-based machine unlearning technique to remove benchmark contamination from code LLMs, with the goal of restoring the validity of benchmark-based evaluation.
The main contributions of this thesis are:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{A controlled contamination simulation.} We designed a controlled contamination scenario where a small code LLM (Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B) is trained on a dataset that intentionally includes a target benchmark (McEval-Hard), allowing us to simulate a clean contamination scenario where the benchmark is explicitly included in training without any pretraining overlap. This provides a clear experimental target for validating the unlearning approach.
	\item \textbf{A benchmark-specific neuron detection pipeline.} We developed a pipeline to identify the neurons most responsible for retaining benchmark-specific knowledge in the contaminated model. This pipeline: uses forward hooks to capture neuron activations patterns on both benchmark and control (non-benchmark) inputs, scores each neuron using Average Precision to quantify how well its activation distinguishes between benchmark and control inputs, and uses a Z-score-based thresholding method to disable the top neurons most associated with benchmark knowledge. A variant of this pipeline targets only the neurons responsible for a subset of tasks the model has demonstrably memorized.
	\item \textbf{A weight masking intervention and evaluation methodology.} We implemented a weight masking intervention that sets the weights of the identified neurons to zero, effectively suppressing their contribution to the model's output. We then evaluated the impact of the intervention on the model's performance using two metrics: functional correctness (accuracy) and structural similarity to canonical solutions (TSED), that together distinguish between true unlearning of benchmark knowledge and over-masking of neurons that degrades general coding capability. We also conducted: a memorization analysis to track the forgetting of specific benchmark problems and the recovery of general coding capabilities, and a cross-benchmark robustness evaluation uing both HumanEval+ and MBPP+ to verify that the masking intervention does not impact too much the performance on other benchmarks.
	\item \textbf{An evaluation across multiple models.} The full pipeline is evaluated on the Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B, as the primary model, as well as on Qwen3-1.7B.
\end{itemize}

The results show that at Z-score threshold Z=8, the intervention achieves a significant reduction in benchmark performance reducing the model's accuracy on McEval-Hard from 39.3\% to 32.25\%.
This reduction matches the performance of the clean baseline while the TSED score confirms that also the structural similarity to canonical solutions is reduced, indicating that the model is not just failing to retrieve memorized solutions but is effectively unlearning benchmark-specific knowledge.
Cross-benchmark evaluation confirms that performance on HumanEval+ and MBPP+ is maintained at Z$\geq$8, validating the specificity of the identified neurons.
\par\vspace{1em}
However, a more detailed analysis reveals important limitations.
The task-level memorization analysis shows that neither the subset-targeting nor the full-benchmark variant is able to forget the 19 problems that the model has memorized without causing collateral damage and recovering the tasks that the clean baseline was solving.
These findings, considering that all the experiments were conducted on a relatively small model and in a controlled contamination scenario, mean that the current neuron-based unlearning approach is not yet sufficient to be claimed as a complete reliable solution to the problem of benchmark contamination and restoring the validity of benchmark-based evaluation in real-world settings.
Instead, this work should be seen as a first investigation step into the potential of neuron-based unlearning, highlighting both its promise and its limitations.

\chapter{Background and Related Work}
This chapter establishes the theoretical and empirical foundations of the thesis. \\
First, we introduce the core concepts underlying benchmark-based evaluation of code LLMs and machine unlearning, and then we survey the relevant literature on code generation benchmarks, unlearning techniques, and data leakage in code LLMs.

\section{Code Generation Benchmarks}
A benchmark is a standardized collection of tasks and evaluation criteria. It allows the research community to compare the capabilities of different models under controlled, reproducible conditions. In code generation, a benchmark generally consists of a prompt (a natural-language description or partial code stub), an expected output (a function or program), and one or more test cases acting as an oracle for functional correctness. The evaluation score is computed by running model-generated code against these test cases.\\

As a running example, consider the following problem prompt:

The primary metric for measuring functional correctness in code generation benchmarks is $pass@k$, as defined by \citet{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2107-03374}. Given a problem, the model generates $n \geq k$ independent code samples. The $pass@k$ score is then computed analytically via a closed-form unbiased estimator over all $n$ samples, rather than generating exactly $k$. Generating more samples than strictly required is important to account for the inherent stochasticity of LLMs: a single sample may fail due to sampling variance rather than a genuine lack of capability, so evaluating over a larger pool yields a more reliable estimate. In practice, $pass@1$ computed with low-temperature sampling is the most commonly reported variant, as it directly reflects the probability that a single generation attempt produces a correct solution.\\

This execution-based evaluation pipeline is the standard approach across code generation benchmarks. It provides an objective, binary signal of correctness — either the code passes all test cases or it does not — avoiding the ambiguity of surface-level textual metrics such as BLEU (\citet{BLUEamethodforautomaticevaluationofmachinetranslation}) or CodeBLEU (\citet{ren2020codebleumethodautomaticevaluation}). These metrics were originally designed for natural language and may assign high scores to syntactically similar but functionally incorrect programs. \citet{Evtikhiev_2023} confirm this limitation empirically, showing that BLEU and CodeBLEU correlate poorly with human judgments of code correctness and can rank functionally incorrect outputs above correct ones.\\

Benchmark evaluation also requires careful test suite design. Inadequate or trivially simple tests can yield inflated accuracy figures that do not reflect true model capability (\citet{zhang2024naturalcodebenchexaminingcodingperformance}; \citet{ouyang2025dscodebenchrealisticbenchmarkdata}; \citet{koohestani2025benchmarkingaimodelssoftware}). For instance, \citet{ouyang2025dscodebenchrealisticbenchmarkdata} reports that DS-1000 provides an average of only 2.1 test cases per problem — a limitation that motivates DSCodeBench's design of 200 test cases per problem to ensure broader coverage of code behavior, including edge cases and intermediate states.\\

A further threat to benchmark validity is data contamination: benchmark problems or their solutions may be present in a model's pretraining or fine-tuning corpus, causing evaluation scores to reflect memorization rather than genuine generalization. This is a particularly acute problem in the code domain, as widely used benchmarks such as HumanEval and MBPP are publicly available across numerous repositories and may have been seen by a model prior to evaluation. Contaminated models perform significantly better on problems encountered during training than on genuinely unseen ones (\citet{riddell2024quantifyingcontaminationevaluatingcode}; \citet{matton2024leakagecodegenerationevaluation}; \citet{zhou2025lessleakbenchinvestigationdataleakage}). These findings collectively highlight how common and impactful benchmark contamination is in code LLMs, which directly motivates the focus of this thesis.

\section{Machine Unlearning}
Understanding machine unlearning requires first understanding how LLMs acquire knowledge. Modern LLMs are trained in two main phases (\citet{brown2020languagemodelsfewshotlearners}).  In the first phase, called pre-training, the model is exposed to a huge amount of raw text and code; trillions of tokens drawn from online resources, code repositories, books. Then, it is trained to predict the next token in a sequence. Through this process, the model encodes: world knowledge, language patterns, and coding conventions into its parameters. Any information present in the pre-training corpus may be memorized, including benchmark problems and their solutions. 
\par\vspace{1em}
In the second phase, the pre-trained model is fine-tuned on smaller, curated datasets to specialize it for a particular task or to align it with human preferences. This takes the form of supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on instruction-response pairs, or reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), which uses human ratings to steer outputs toward being more helpful, harmless, and honest (\citet{ouyang2022traininglanguagemodelsfollow}). For code-specific models, fine-tuning typically involves additional training on curated code datasets, commit histories, or synthesized instruction-following examples. This pipeline is now standard for building capable code LLMs such as CodeLlama, DeepSeek-Coder, and Qwen-Coder (\citet{rozière2024codellamaopenfoundation}; \citet{hui2024qwen25codertechnicalreport}). 
\par\vspace{1em}
The knowledge absorbed during the pre-training phase are embedded in the weights of the model and it is not easy to remove them.
Fine-tuning on new data is not a  guarantee that previously memorized information is erased — (\citet{hong2024dissectingfinetuningunlearninglarge}) show through activation patching experiments that fine-tuning-based unlearning methods merely alter the model's knowledge retrieval process rather than genuinely removing the knowledge from its parameters, which remains largely recoverable. This is the problem that machine unlearning addresses.
\par\vspace{1em}
Machine unlearning (MU) refers to the specific removal of some training data or knowledge from a trained model, without retraining from scratch. Originally motivated by data privacy regulations (GDPR's right to be forgotten \citet{7163042}), MU has expanded to address copyright removal, safety alignment, bias mitigation, and hallucination reduction in LLMs (\citet{liu2024rethinkingmachineunlearninglarge}). In the unlearning literature, the data to be removed is often referred to as the "forget set", while the data whose knowledge should be preserved is called the "retain set". The unlearning process must ensure that the model's performance on the retain set remains intact while effectively erasing knowledge from the forget set.
\par\vspace{1em}
Exact unlearning — retraining from scratch on the dataset with the target data removed — is the most reliable approach but is computationally infeasible for large models. Research has therefore concentrated on approximate unlearning methods that modify a trained model post-hoc. These methods must balance two competing objectives: unlearning efficacy, meaning that targeted knowledge is effectively erased, and utility preservation, meaning that general model capabilities outside the unlearning scope remain intact.
\par\vspace{1em}
Gradient Ascent (GA) is one of the most common used unlearning approaches. Rather than minimizing the prediction loss, as in standard training, GA maximizes the loss on the data to be forgotten (\citet{yao2024largelanguagemodelunlearning}). However, during the optimization, GA alone can be sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters, which can lead to unlearning failures as catastrophic collapse  (\citet{liu2024rethinkingmachineunlearninglarge}). This has given rise to several improved variants.
\par\vspace{1em}
Gradient Difference (GradDiff) is the combination of GA on the forget set with gradient descent on the retain set and a KL divergence regularization term to minimize the divergence between the unlearned model and the original model on a reference dataset, preventing catastrophic deterioration of general capability (\citet{hong2024dissectingfinetuningunlearninglarge}).
\par\vspace{1em}
Negative Preference Optimization (NPO) treats the forget set exclusively as negative examples: it turns the unlearning problem into a minimization problem mitigating the catastrophic collapse compared to GA (\citet{liu2024rethinkingmachineunlearninglarge}; \citet{jia2025waglestrategicweightattribution}).
\par\vspace{1em}
Preference Optimization (PO)  takes the opposite direction from NPO: instead of treating forget data as negative examples, it introduces targeted unlearning responses ("I don't know") as positive examples, producing a bounded forget loss (\citet{jia2025waglestrategicweightattribution}).
Another variant transforms GA into a gradient descent approach by minimizing the likelihood of predictions on relabeled forgetting data, replacing the original responses with generic or uninformative alternatives (\citet{yao2024largelanguagemodelunlearning}; \citet{liu2024rethinkingmachineunlearninglarge}).
\par\vspace{1em}
A related approach is Task Arithmetic (\citet{ilharco2023editingmodelstaskarithmetic}; \citet{cai2026perparametertaskarithmeticunlearning}): a task vector is defined as the element-wise difference between a fine-tuned model's weights and the original pre-trained model's weights.
Negating this vector and subtracting it from the pre-trained model degrades performance on the target task while leaving unrelated capabilities intact.
\par\vspace{1em}
A distinct line of work frames unlearning through mechanistic interpretability. Rather than modifying all model parameters globally, these methods first localize the neurons or weight subsets responsible for storing the target knowledge, then restrict modifications to those components. This limits collateral damage to unrelated capabilities and improves both unlearning efficacy and utility preservation (\citet{jia2025waglestrategicweightattribution}; \citet{pochinkov2024dissectinglanguagemodelsmachine}). This neuron-based paradigm is the foundational principle of the approach developed in this thesis.
While gradient-based methods are more flexible and can be applied to any unlearning target, they risk degrading unrelated capabilities. In contrast, neuron-based methods target only the parameters responsible for the knowledge to be removed, offering a more surgical intervention that can achieve better unlearning efficacy while preserving general utility. This makes neuron-based unlearning particularly well-suited for the code domain, where the granularity of knowledge is often finer and more localized than in natural language tasks.

\section{Related Work}
Below, we present a survey of previous studies related to code generation benchmarks, machine unlearning techniques, and data leakage in code LLMs.

\subsection{Evaluation of LLMs on Code Generation Tasks}
HumanEval (\citet{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2107-03374}) is arguably the most widely adopted benchmark for evaluating code generation. It consists of 164 hand-crafted Python problems, each defined by a function signature, a docstring, and a hidden test suite. Solutions are evaluated via execution against unit tests. MBPP (Mostly Basic Python Problems) (\citet{austin2021programsynthesislargelanguage}), is a crowdsourced collection of 974 Python programming problems of varying difficulty with a natural-language description and on average three test cases (\citet{ouyang2025dscodebenchrealisticbenchmarkdata}; \citet{koohestani2025benchmarkingaimodelssoftware}). Despite their popularity both benchmarks suffer from well-documented limitations: they are narrowly Python-centric, contain predominantly algorithmic and basic programming problems (96.9\% and 89.5\% of their content, respectively  (\citet{zhang2024naturalcodebenchexaminingcodingperformance})), and provide limited test coverage that makes evaluation scores statistically unstable (\citet{koohestani2025benchmarkingaimodelssoftware}).
To overcome the limitations of HumanEval and MBPP, a range of more comprehensive and challenging benchmarks has proposed by the research community. Direct improvements include: HumanEval+ and MBPP+ (\citet{liu2023codegeneratedchatgptreally}) that extend the original benchmarks with more problems and stronger test suites reducing the risk of false positives, due to weak test coverage.
\par\vspace{1em}
NaturalCodeBench (NCB) (\citet{zhang2024naturalcodebenchexaminingcodingperformance}) contains 402 problems drawn from real coding online services across six domains (software engineering, data science, algorithms, systems administration, AI systems, and front-end development).
Differs from HumanEval and MBPP since it features multi-file format and it is evaluated in an executable Docker environment, to have a more realistic setting.
Notably, a performance mismatch study showed that many models ranked highly on HumanEval perform substantially worse on NCB, suggesting overfitting to the former.
\par\vspace{1em}
DSCodeBench (\citet{ouyang2025dscodebenchrealisticbenchmarkdata}) contains 1,000 problems sourced from real GitHub repositories and covering ten popular Python libraries (NumPy, Pandas, SciPy, Scikit-learn, TensorFlow, PyTorch, Matplotlib, Seaborn, Keras, and LightGBM). and targets the data science domain specifically. Each problem has a solution code of 22.5 lines and a rich test suites of 200 cases, on average, providing significantly stronger evaluation than prior data science benchmarks such as DS-1000.
\par\vspace{1em}
Other notable specialized benchmarks include: BigCodeBench (\citet{zhuo2025bigcodebenchbenchmarkingcodegeneration}), that targets diverse function calls across 62 libraries; SWE-Bench (\citet{jimenez2024swebenchlanguagemodelsresolve}), that evaluates practical software engineering by asking models to fix real GitHub issues; EditBench (\citet{chi2025editbenchevaluatingllmabilities}), that assesses instructed code editing over 540 real-world problems; GitChameleon 2.0 (\citet{misra2025gitchameleon20evaluatingai}), that evaluates version-conditioned code generation across Python library updates; and LiveCodeBench (\citet{jain2024livecodebenchholisticcontaminationfree}), a dynamic benchmark that continuously aggregates new problems from competitive programming platforms specifically to avoid overfitting and reduce the risk of data contamination (\citet{koohestani2025benchmarkingaimodelssoftware}). For multi-language evaluation, benchmarks such as HumanEval-X (\citet{zheng2024codegeexpretrainedmodelcode}), MultiPL-E (\citet{cassano2022multiplescalableextensibleapproach}), XLCoST (\citet{koohestani2025benchmarkingaimodelssoftware}), and McEval (\citet{chai2024mcevalmassivelymultilingualcode}) do not contain only Python-centric problems but extend their scope to dozens of programming languages.
\par\vspace{1em}
McEval (\citet{chai2024mcevalmassivelymultilingualcode}) is a massively multilingual code evaluation benchmark covering 40 programming languages and comprising over 16,000 code generation problems, each accompanied by a natural-language description and executable test cases. In this thesis, we specifically use McEval-Hard, a challenging subset of McEval that focuses on Python problems of above-average difficulty. McEval-Hard comprises 227 problems, each defined by a function signature, a natural-language docstring, and a suite of executable test cases. Its difficulty and manageable size make it well-suited for controlled contamination experiments: it is challenging enough that performance differences between a clean and a contaminated model are visible, while small enough to allow multiple training and evaluation runs within a reasonable computational budget. For these reasons, McEval-Hard serves as the primary benchmark throughout all experiments in this thesis.
\par\vspace{1em}
A comprehensive survey (\citet{koohestani2025benchmarkingaimodelssoftware}) catalogues hundreds of code benchmarks organized along multiple dimensions including: task type (generation, summarization, translation, repair, editing), programming language, and evaluation protocol, showing that the benchmarks described above are widely used and cited evaluation standards in the code LLM community. 
\par\vspace{1em}
Their popularity introduces a significant risk: because these benchmarks are publicly available, they may have been included in the pretraining data of many models, leading to data contamination and inflated performance scores that do not reflect true generalization, rather memorization. This is a critical problem that motivates the focus of this thesis: how can we ensure fair and accurate evaluation of code LLMs on benchmarks that may have been contaminated? The solution we explore is to apply machine unlearning techniques to remove benchmark-specific knowledge from contaminated models, thereby restoring the validity of benchmark-based evaluation without the need for costly retraining or new benchmark creation.
This risk concern is not hypothetical: as discussed in the following sections, studies have empirically confirmed the presence of benchmark data in LLMs training corpora, motivating the decontamination approach explored in this thesis.

\subsection{Data Leakage in Code LLMs}
\citet{Sallou_2024} are among the first to systematically document this risk in the software engineering context, identifing three key threats to the validity of LLM-based software engineering experiments:  it is often unknown what data closed-source models were trained on, the boundary between training and evaluation data is frequently unclear, and results may not be reproducible across different model versions. They provide a striking illustration: ChatGPT-3.5, though not fine-tuned for bug-fixing tasks, exhibits precise knowledge of specific bugs in the Defects4J dataset — almost certainly because descriptions of those bugs appeared in its pretraining corpus. This means the model is effectively evaluated on data it has already seen, and its results may not generalize to code it has never encountered.
\par\vspace{1em}
\citet{riddell2024quantifyingcontaminationevaluatingcode} perform a systematic study of overlap between popular code generation benchmarks and two large pretraining corpora (The Pile and The Stack). Using both surface-level string matching and semantic-level embedding similarity, they find substantial overlap: 12.2\% of HumanEval problems are present in The Pile, and 18.9\% in The Stack; for MBPP the figures are 6.9\% and 32.2\%, respectively. Critically, models perform significantly better on the contaminated subset of problems than on the non-contaminated subset, confirming that contamination inflates reported scores.  By analyzing the factors that modulate effects of contamination, emerged that larger models and shorter problems are more susceptible to memorization.
\par\vspace{1em}
\citet{matton2024leakagecodegenerationevaluation} distinguish three contamination ways: (1) direct leakage, where benchmark problems, both in original or paraphrased forms, are directly present in training corpora (e.g. every HumanEval prompt appears at least 43 times in public GitHub repositories); (2) indirect leakage, where large-scale code synthesis pipelines produce training examples highly similar to benchmark problems,  through synthetic data; and (3) overfitting through checkpoint selection, where repeated evaluation on public benchmarks during development leads to selection bias towards model versions that perform well on leaked data. The authors introduce LBPP (Less Basic Python Problems), a benchmark of 161 carefully crafted problems designed to be free of contamination, and demonstrate that leading models achieve pass@1 scores up to 43% lower on LBPP than on HumanEval — a striking indication of the extent to which reported HumanEval performance reflects overfitting rather than true coding capability.
\par\vspace{1em}
\citet{zhou2025lessleakbenchinvestigationdataleakage} conduct the most comprehensive leakage audit to date, examining data leakage across 83 software engineering benchmarks (spanning: Python, Java, C, C++, and other languages). The methodology combines automatic detection (MinHash + Locality-Sensitive Hashing on n-gram representations of code) with manual verification by experienced developers to eliminate false positives. The study finds an average leakage ratio of 4.8\% across Python SE benchmarks, but with striking variation (e.g. SWE-Bench shows 8.7\% leakage). Notably, even benchmarks considered 'safe' such as HumanEval (1.8\%) and MBPP (0.4\%) show non-negligible overlap.
\par\vspace{1em}
Several methods have been proposed to detect whether a model has been trained on a given benchmark. \citet{shi2024detectingpretrainingdatalarge} propose a Min-K\% Prob method that exploits the observation that a model assigns higher minimum token probabilities to memorized text than to novel text. \citet{zawalski2025detectingdatacontaminationllms} show that a model's ability to complete partially revealed benchmark problems — using only a few in-context examples as cues — serves as evidence of prior exposure. \citet{stoehr2024localizingparagraphmemorizationlanguage} further show that memorization of multi-sentence passages can be traced to specific, localized regions of the model via activation analysis, providing mechanistic evidence for the viability of targeted unlearning.
\par\vspace{1em}
Despite these advances in detection, removal of detected contamination remains an open problem. Filtering training corpora is error-prone and incomplete; retraining is expensive; and alternative benchmark creation is a race that will eventually be lost as models train on ever-larger internet scrapes. These limitations suggest that preventing contamination at the source is not a viable long-term solution, motivating the use of post-hoc techniques — such as machine unlearning — to neutralize its effects directly in the model.

\subsection{Machine Unlearning}
An increasing number of work has explored machine unlearning for LLMs. \citet{yao2024largelanguagemodelunlearning} introduce a gradient ascent-based framework targeting harmful and privacy-violating content in LLMs. \citet{liu2024rethinkingmachineunlearninglarge} provide a comprehensive rethinking of the field, surveying and unifying gradient-based, PEFT-based, localization-informed, and influence-function approaches under a common framework. \citet{wang2024llmunlearninglossadjustment} propose FLAT, a loss adjustment method that performs unlearning using only the forget data, without requiring access to a retain set or reference model. \citet{hong2024dissectingfinetuningunlearninglarge} dissect the internal mechanics of fine-tuning-based unlearning, showing through activation patching and parameter restoration experiments that these methods alter how knowledge is retrieved rather than truly erasing it. \citet{ilharco2023editingmodelstaskarithmetic} demonstrate that negating a fine-tuning task vector can effectively suppress a learned capability while largely preserving general model performance.
\par\vspace{1em}
While these approaches cover a broad spectrum of unlearning techniques, the focus of this thesis is on neuron-based and localization-informed methods, which offer the most targeted and precise form of knowledge removal. Rather than applying gradient updates across all model parameters — which risks degrading unrelated capabilities — these approaches first identify the specific model components responsible for storing the target knowledge, and then restrict modifications to those components only. We now review the most relevant works in this line.
\par\vspace{1em}
\citet{kojima2024multilingualabilitydecoderbasedpretrained} provide the foundational insight for this thesis. They demonstrate that language-specific behaviors in multilingual LLMs can be attributed to a sparse subset of neurons in the feed-forward network layers. By analyzing activation patterns conditioned on language-specific inputs, they identify these neurons and show that patching (zeroing out) them at inference time achieves targeted capability removal without broadly degrading model performance. 
\par\vspace{1em}
\citet{pochinkov2024dissectinglanguagemodelsmachine} introduce a post-hoc approach where, to identify neurons contributing to a specific capability (e.g. coding ability or toxic behavior), they used the influence functions computed after training  — and to remove those neurons while leaving the rest of the network intact they used unstructured pruning. This work demonstrates that capability-level unlearning can be achieved through surgical weight removal without any gradient-based optimization.
\par\vspace{1em}
\citet{jia2025waglestrategicweightattribution} propose WAGLE, which extends the localization idea to weight-level attribution using bi-level optimization. Rather than relying on simple magnitude-based pruning or gradient-based saliency, WAGLE derives closed-form attribution scores that jointly consider the unlearning objective and the utility retention objective. Experiments on the TOFU and WMDP benchmarks show that restricting gradient updates to WAGLE-selected weights (typically 80-95\% density) consistently improves unlearning efficacy while maintaining utility, outperforming dense model-based baselines.
\par\vspace{1em}
\citet{cai2026perparametertaskarithmeticunlearning} further refine the task vector negation approach through per-parameter scaling. They address a key limitation of naive task vector subtraction: because the forget target and other knowledge may be coupled in the same parameters, a uniform negation risks corrupting retained capabilities. Their per-parameter scaling allows finer-grained control over which parts of the model are affected by the unlearning operation.
\par\vspace{1em}
Turning to unlearning applied specifically to code language models, \citet{chu2026scrubitouterasingsensitivememorization} address the problem of sensitive memorization — including leaked API keys, email addresses, and IP addresses. They introduce CodeEraser, a selective unlearning framework that performs gradient ascent on sensitive segments of code while applying KL divergence regularization on non-sensitive contexts, minimizing collateral damage to the model's broader coding utility. Results on CodeParrot, CodeGen, and InCoder show that memorized sensitive content can be effectively erased while preserving benchmark performance on HumanEval.
\par\vspace{1em}
\citet{jiang2025largelanguagemodelunlearning} focus on unlearning copyrighted code and highlight the tension between removing memorized content and preserving coding ability. This work underscores that, in the code domain, the granularity and precision of the unlearning target are especially important: overly broad unlearning degrades general-purpose coding capabilities, while overly narrow unlearning may not fully suppress memorized knowledge — a challenge that neuron-level targeting is well-positioned to address.

\chapter{Study Design}
This chapter describes the end-to-end design of our approach for benchmarking unlearning in the evaluation of code LLMs. The core idea is to identify the specific neurons inside a contaminated model that are responsible for memorizing benchmark content, and to disable them — without any retraining. The pipeline is organized into six stages: dataset construction, model training and contamination simulation, baseline inference, benchmark-specific neuron detection, neuron masking and unlearning evaluation, and finally a memorization analysis to quantify the effectiveness of the intervention.

\section{Evaluated Code Generation Benchmarks}
\label{sec:benchmarks}
The experimental pipeline requires two distinct types of benchmarks: one that serves as the
contamination target, and others that act as independent probes for general coding capability.
Each plays a different but complementary role in validating the approach.
 
\paragraph{McEval-Hard (contamination target).}
The primary benchmark used throughout this work is McEval-Hard, a challenging
subset derived from McEval (\citet{chai2024mcevalmassivelymultilingualcode}). McEval-Hard
is constructed by selecting the hardest tasks from the McEval collection and translating
them into Python prompts, yielding 227 problems of above-average difficulty. Each problem
consists of a function signature, a natural-language docstring, and a suite of executable test
cases (shown in Figure~\ref{fig:mceval_example}). Importantly, each problem also comes with
a \emph{canonical solution}: a reference implementation produced by prompting multiple LLMs
and then manually reviewing and correcting the generated outputs, ensuring solution quality.
This canonical solution is used in our work to compute structural similarity metrics between
model outputs and the ground-truth implementation.
 
McEval-Hard is chosen as the contamination target for three reasons. First, and most
critically, it provides a clean experimental foundation: McEval-Hard is not publicly available
as a standalone dataset, and the original McEval benchmark was released in June 2024---after the training data cutoff of Qwen2.5-Coder, which is estimated
at around early 2024 (\citet{hui2024qwen25codertechnicalreport}). This means the model
cannot have encountered this benchmark during pretraining, making it possible to
distinguish genuine contamination effects (introduced by us, in a controlled way) from
pre-existing memorization. Without this guarantee, it would be impossible to know whether
any observed performance inflation was due to our simulated leakage or to prior exposure.
For Qwen3.5-2B, whose training cutoff is more recent, prior exposure remains unlikely given
that McEval-Hard is not available in the public literature. Second, the difficulty of the problems
ensures that any performance gap between a clean and a contaminated model is clearly
visible, rather than being masked by problems that any capable model would solve
regardless. Third, its manageable size (227 problems) makes it easier to include in a ``leaked corpus`` and to
evaluate repeatedly across multiple experimental runs within a reasonable computational
time.
 
\paragraph{HumanEval+ and MBPP+ (robustness probes).}
To verify that the unlearning intervention does not impair general coding capability,
we use HumanEval+ and MBPP+ (\citet{liu2023codegeneratedchatgptreally}) as independent
evaluation benchmarks. These are strengthened versions of HumanEval and MBPP respectively,
extended with denser test suites to reduce the risk of false positives. HumanEval and MBPP are the most widely adopted
benchmarks for code generation evaluation, used as standard reference points across a large
body of work on code LLMs (\citet{hui2024qwen25codertechnicalreport}, \citet{li2026manifoldguidedattentionsteering},
\citet{ouyang2026executionstaticanalysisrewardshintconditioned}). Being well-established and
relatively accessible benchmarks, they also serve a secondary diagnostic purpose: if the
masking intervention were to cause substantial degradation even on these simpler problems,
it would signal that the ablation has damaged the model's general coding ability well beyond
the intended unlearning scope. Crucially, neither benchmark is used at any stage of the
neuron detection or masking process; they are exclusively reserved for post-intervention
evaluation.

\section{Dataset Construction}
\label{sec:datasets}
Here we describe the construction of the datasets used in this work. The experimental pipeline relies on three distinct datasets: two training datasets that are used to produce the clean and contaminated model variants, and a control dataset that is used during the neuron detection phase to isolate benchmark-specific internal representations. Each dataset is carefully designed to serve its specific role in the pipeline, and the design choices behind each one are motivated below.
 
\paragraph{PL-Only dataset.}
The first training dataset, which we refer to as the \emph{PL-Only} dataset, is designed to
simulate a realistic model update on code ---  a common scenario in which a model is fine-tuned on a new corpus of code data, for example to improve performance on a specific domain or to incorporate more recent code.
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pl-only} it is constructed by sampling 2,000 code instances from \texttt{bigcode/the-stack}
(\citet{kocetkov2022stack3tbpermissively}), a large-scale code dataset sourced from public GitHub repositories.
Instances are sampled from eight programming languages: Python, Java, JavaScript, C, C++, Go, Rust, and C\#.
\par\vspace{1em}
The multi-language composition is a deliberate design choice. Real-world training corpora are
rarely Python-only, and a model update that happens to include accidental benchmark leakage would likely be a multi-language update rather than a Python-specific one.
By training on a diverse set of languages, we ensure that any performance advantage observed in the
contaminated model later cannot be explained by incidental exposure to Python code alone, but rather must reflect genuine memorization of the benchmark content. 
Additionally, the diversity prevents the model from overfitting to a single language during fine-tuning, which would make it harder to detect the specific effects of benchmark contamination. By contrast, a Python-only training dataset would be more likely to produce a model that performs well on Python benchmarks simply due to improved general Python coding ability, rather than due to memorization of specific benchmark problems.
The PL-Only dataset contains no benchmark problems by construction, making it the clean baseline against which
contamination effects are measured.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{img/pl-only.png}
\caption{Composition of the PL-Only training dataset.}
\label{fig:pl-only}
\end{figure}
 
\paragraph{Leaked dataset.}
The second training dataset, which we refer to as the Leaked dataset, is constructed
to simulate an accidental data contamination scenario: the kind of accidental leakage that
can occur when a benchmark is publicly available and gets scraped into a training corpus
without explicit filtering. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:leaked} it is formed by taking the full PL-Only dataset and appending to it
the 227 problems from McEval-Hard. The result is a mixed corpus of 2,227 instances, structurally identical to
the PL-Only dataset except for the presence of benchmark data.
\par\vspace{1em} 
Keeping the two datasets otherwise identical is important for the validity of the controlled
experiment: any behavioral difference between a model trained on PL-Only and one trained
on the Leaked dataset can be attributed solely to benchmark exposure, rather than to
differences in data volume, language distribution, or training dynamics. A model fine-tuned
on the Leaked dataset is expected to achieve inflated scores on McEval-Hard as a direct
consequence of having memorized benchmark content during training --- and it is precisely
this inflated model that the neuron masking intervention aims to correct.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{img/leaked.png}
\caption{Composition of the Leaked training dataset.}
\label{fig:leaked}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Control dataset.}
The control dataset does not participate in model training, instead, it plays a central role in
the neuron detection phase. Its purpose is to provide a distribution of neuronal activations non-benchmark Python code,
against which the benchmark-specific neuronal activations of the contaminated model can be
compared. The intuition is simple: a neuron that fires consistently higher on benchmark
inputs instead of a structurally similar but content-different code inputs, is likely encoding
benchmark-specific knowledge rather than general coding ability.
\par\vspace{1em} 
The control dataset is sourced from \texttt{bigcode/the-stack} (\citet{kocetkov2022stack3tbpermissively}), drawing exclusively from
Python. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:control} a pool of 100,000 Python instances, from different domains, is assembled and shuffled.
A subset of 10,000 samples are retained as the final control set. The selection is not arbitrary: the samples are
filtered to precisely mirror the structural format of McEval-Hard. Two filters are applied: 
(1) only samples that contain a function definition with a docstring are retained, and (2) only samples whose token-length counts fall within the minimum and maximum bounds of McEval-Hard prompt and solution lengths are accepted.
This structural choice is essential. Without it, any observed difference in neuronal activation between benchmark and control inputs could
simply reflect differences in code format, prompt length, or presentation style rather than
genuine benchmark memorization. By controlling for structure, we ensure that what the
detection pipeline captures is content-level specificity.

\section{Experimental Procedure and Data Analysis}
With the datasets constructed, we now have the necessary components to carry out the full experimental procedure:
how the models are trained, how benchmark-specific neurons are identified inside the contaminated model, how
those neurons are disabled through masking, and how the effect of the intervention is measured. Each step
builds directly on the previous one, forming an end-to-end pipeline from model training to
unlearning evaluation.
 
\subsection{Model Training} 
The first step of the pipeline is to produce the two model variants described conceptually in
the previous section: a clean baseline trained on the PL-Only corpus, and a contaminated model
trained on the Leaked corpus. Both models are trained from the same Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B
base checkpoint using identical hyperparameters but different training data.
This controlled setup ensures that the only difference between the two models is the presence of benchmark data in the training corpus of the contaminated model, and that any observed performance difference can be attributed to this factor alone. 
By contrast, if we were to train two models from different base checkpoints, or with different hyperparameters, we would introduce multiple variables that could affect performance independently of benchmark exposure, making it impossible to isolate the effect of contamination.
\par\vspace{1em} 
Both runs share the configuration reported in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}. 
The training pipeline follows a chunk-and-tokenize strategy: each code instance is tokenized and split
into non-overlapping chunks of up to 2,048 tokens, with beginning-of-sequence and
end-of-sequence tokens respectively. 
Training is carried out for 10 epochs in both cases, and the same checkpoint selection strategy is applied: 
checkpoints are selected based on convergence of the training loss to avoid overfitting, and to ensure that the model has had sufficient opportunity to memorize the benchmark content in the case of the Leaked dataset.

\paragraph{Baseline model (PL-Only).}
The baseline model is obtained by training Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B on the PL-Only dataset.
As described in section~\ref{sec:datasets}, this dataset was designed to simulate a realistic model update on code --- a
continued fine-tuning on a clean, multilingual corpus with no benchmark content.
The checkpoint at epoch 2 is selected as the final baseline, corresponding to the point of
training loss convergence. This model represents the scenario we want to use as a reference:
a code LLM that has undergone a training update but has never been exposed to
McEval-Hard. Any performance the model achieves on McEval-Hard can be attributed to genuine generalization rather than memorization, and it serves as the clean baseline against which the contaminated model and the unlearning intervention are later evaluated.
 
\paragraph{Leaked model (contaminated).}
The contaminated model is obtained by training the same base checkpoint on the Leaked
dataset --- the PL-Only corpus with the 227 McEval-Hard problems. As described in section~\ref{sec:datasets},
this represents a simulated but realistic scenario where a model update accidentally incorporates benchmark data,
leading to an unintended leakage that can occur when benchmarks are publicly available and scraped. 
The checkpoint at epoch 3 is selected based on training loss convergence. 
This model is expected to achieve an high evaluation scores on the benchmark as a direct consequence of memorization --- and it is
precisely the gap between this model and the clean baseline that the unlearning intervention
aims to close.
 
\paragraph{Post-training instruct weight transfer.}
At this point both trained models have acquired the code knowledge from their respective training datasets, but they lack the instruction-following capability required to perform zero-shot code generation from natural-language prompts. This is a critical limitation, as it prevents us from evaluating their performance on the benchmarks in a realistic setting.
The straightforward solution would be to fine-tune both models on instruction-response pairs, but this would risk overwriting the code knowledge we just instilled, and would introduce an additional training stage that could interact with the contamination signal in unpredictable ways.
Instead, we adopt a weight transfer approach based on the task arithmetic framework
\citep{ilharco2023editing}, which has shown that model capabilities can be composed
and transferred by operating directly in weight space, without any additional training.
\par\vspace{1em} 
The procedure, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:weight_transfer}, computes the weight
difference between the official Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct model and the
Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B base model, and adds this difference to the weights of each
trained model. Formally, for each weight matrix $W$:
\[
  W_{\textit{final}} = W_{\textit{fine-tuned}} + (W_{\textit{instruct}} - W_{\textit{base}})
\]
The result, leaves the code knowledge acquired during fine-tuning intact --- since the delta is added on top of the fine-tuned weights rather than replacing
them while granting the model the ability to follow chat-style prompts and produce
structured code completions. Both the baseline and the leaked model undergo this step,
producing the final pair of models that are evaluated throughout the rest of the pipeline.

($W_{\text{instruct}} - W_{\text{base}}$) is extracted from the official Instruct model

\subsection{Benchmark-Specific Neuron Detection} 
\label{sec:thresholding}
With the two model variants created and the post-training weight transfer done, we now have a contaminated model that is expected to perform well on McEval-Hard due to memorization, and a clean baseline that serves as a reference for genuine generalization.
The next step is to access the internal representations of the contaminated model and identify which neurons are responsible for the memorization of benchmark content, and therefore for the inflated benchmark performance.
The core intuition is straightforward: if a neuron has memorized benchmark content, it should respond more strongly to benchmark inputs than to structurally similar but content-different code inputs.
The control dataset constructed in Section~\ref{sec:datasets} was designed precisely to make this comparison clean.
The detection pipeline proceeds through four steps: activation extraction, expertise scoring, statistical thresholding, and neuron isolation.
 
\subsubsection{Activation Extraction via Forward Hooks} 
The first step of the detection pipeline is to extract the activation patterns of every neuron in the contaminated model in response to both benchmark and control inputs, without modifying the model's architecture 
or interfering with its forward pass. The full process is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:activation_extraction}.
\par\vspace{1em}
In order to achieve this, we use PyTorch forward pre-hooks, which allow us to capture the input activations of the down-projection layer during the model's forward pass.
Due to length variation across sequences, we apply max-pooling over the sequence dimension to reduce each sequence to a single activation vector per neuron, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:activation_extraction}.
This layer is chosen for several reasons: it is the final layer of the feed-forward network, it projects the intermediate MLP representations back into the model's residual stream,
and its input therefore captures the full activation pattern of every individual neuron in the feed-forward network. 
Additionaly, it is the layer targeted in literature in neuron-based unlearning work (\citet{kojima2024multilingualabilitydecoderbasedpretrained}), giving to our detection a direct connection to the subsequent masking step.
\par\vspace{1em}
During this process, both the benchmark and control datasets are passed through the contaminated model in: batches of 128 sequences, tokenized to a maximum length of 512 tokens. 
Since sequences vary in length and we need a single scalar per neuron per input, we apply max-pooling over the sequence dimension --- keeping the maximum activation value across all token positions. 
The reason for this choice is that a neuron that memorizes benchmark content may only fire strongly at specific positions in the input (e.g. when it encounters a particular function name or code pattern), and averaging over the sequence could reduce the signal by diluting these strong activations with many low-activation positions.
The result is a matrix that contains the maximum activation value for each neuron in the down-projection layer, for every input example in both datasets. This matrix serves as the raw data for the subsequent expertise scoring step, providing a complete map of how each neuron responds to benchmark and control inputs.

\subsubsection{Expertise Scoring via Average Precision}
Once the activation matrix is obtained, the next step is to score each neuron
on how well it distinguishes benchmark inputs from control inputs. 
If a neuron activates strongly on all Python code, regardless of whether it is from the benchmark or the control dataset, is not useful.
We are looking for neurons that activate more on benchmark code than on general code of the same structure and length.
\par\vspace{1em}
To quantify this, we use the Average Precision (AP) score.
AP is a widely used standard metric in information retrieval and binary classification tasks, representing the area under the precision-recall curve.
We treat the activation values of each neuron $n$ in each layer $l$ as prediction scores in a binary classification problem: 
benchmark activations receive a positive label (1) and control activations receive a negative label (0). 
If a neuron consistently assigns higher activation values to benchmark inputs than to control inputs, it will achieve high precision at every recall level, yielding an AP score close to 1.0.
Instead, a neuron that cannot distinguish the two datasets will produce a precision-recall curve close to a flat line, yielding an AP score effectively around 0.5 in a balanced comparison --- the baseline
ratio of positive to total samples. We choose AP over simpler alternatives because it is threshold-free and robust to the heavy-tailed distribution of neuron activations:
it evaluates the full ranking of activations rather than relying on a single summary statistic, producing a complete per-neuron expertise map of the contaminated model.
 
\subsubsection{Statistical Thresholding and Neuron Isolation}
The expertise map gives us an AP score for every neuron in the network. 
Now, the question is: which neurons are genuinely benchmark-specific, and which ones just score slightly
above chance by coincidence?
\par\vspace{1em}
A naive approach would be to set a fixed threshold --- for example, flagging all neurons with $\text{AP} > 0.7$. 
However, this approach is not the best choice for two reasons. 
First, it doesn not generalise across models: varying architectures, training data, and parameter counts produce different distributions of AP scores, making a fixed threshold unreliable.
Second, since the control dataset is much larger than the benchmark dataset (10,000 samples vs 227), any given neuron has more opportunities to activate on control inputs than on benchmark inputs.
This imbalance can lead to a skewed distribution of AP scores, where even neurons that are not genuinely benchmark-specific may achieve moderately high AP scores simply due to the larger number of control samples, making a fixed threshold even more unreliable.
\par\vspace{1em}
The thresholding we adopt is a Z-score based threshold. 
The Z-score is a standard statistical tool used to identify outliers relative to a distribution.
It measures how many standard deviations a value lies above the mean of the population. 
For a distribution with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$, the Z-score of a value $x$ is defined as $(x - \mu) / \sigma$.
The higher the Z-score, the more anomalous the value is relative to the distribution, far enough that it
is unlikely to have arisen by chance. Applied to our setting, we compute the global mean
$\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$ of all AP scores across the entire network, and define
the threshold $T$ as:
\[
  T = \mu + Z \cdot \sigma
\]
Any neuron whose AP score exceeds $T$ is flagged as benchmark-specific. 
By setting the threshold to the actual distribution of AP scores in the model instead of an arbitrary
fixed value, this approach adapts to different models and scales correctly with the size of the control dataset.
A neuron that achieves a global AP score that is only moderately above chance will have a low Z-score and will not be flagged; 
only neurons that are genuine outliers --- those whose benchmark specificity is statistically anomalous relative to the rest of the distribution --- are flagged.
\par\vspace{1em}
In our experiments, we evaluate $Z$ values ranging from 6 to 10, oscillating from more
aggressive (lower $Z$, more neurons flagged) to more conservative (higher $Z$, fewer
neurons flagged). The sensitivity of the unlearning outcome to this hyperparameter is
analysed in the results. The indices and layer mappings of all flagged neurons are saved for
the subsequent masking step and analysis.

In this variant, the target dataset consists exclusively of the memorized problems rather than

pure memorization over that subset of tasks rather than all the benchmark problems.

\subsection{Neuron Masking and Unlearning Evaluation}
\label{sec:masking}
At this point in the pipeline we have identified a list of neuron indices --- one per transformer layer --- that has been identified in the previous step as being responsible for encoding benchmark-specific knowledge. These are the neurons that, when activated, contribute to the model's ability to recall and reproduce benchmark solutions from memory rather than solving them through generalization.
The next question is what to do with these neurons. 
The ideal next step would be to disable them, effectively silencing their contribution to the model's output.
The applied intervention is relatively simple: we zero out the weights of each flagged neuron in the down-projection layer. 
The idea is that a neuron identified as encoding benchmark knowledge can be silenced/disabled by removing its contribution to the model's output, without touching any other part of the model. 
 
\subsubsection{Neuron Masking Via Weight Ablation} 
Concretely, for each flagged neuron $n$ in layer $l$, we set the corresponding column of
the down-projection weight matrix to zero.
By zeroing an entire column, the output of that neuron is forced to be zero regardless of its input activation --- its output is always zero.
This is a direct, surgical intervention: it doesn't require gradient computation, nor additional
training data, nor iterative optimization. 
The process is applied in a single pass over the saved neuron indices, taking only seconds. 
After zeroing the weights, a verification routine checks that the intervention was applied correctly, confirming that all targeted columns are strictly zero before inference begins, ensuring the integrity of the intervention.
\par\vspace{1em}
What makes this approach appealing is its simplicity and locality. Since we are only modifying the columns corresponding to flagged neurons, every other weight in the model remains completely untouched.
The evaluation that follows will determine whether this targeted ablation is sufficient to suppress the memorization of benchmark content, and whether it does so without broadly degrading the model's general coding capabilities.
 
\subsubsection{Inference Pipeline} 
To measure the effect of the neuron masking intervention, we need to evaluate the performance of all model variants --- the baseline (PL-Only),
the contaminated (Leaked), and each masked variant at different Z-score thresholds --- on McEval-Hard using the same inference pipeline.
This ensures that if any difference in performance between models is observed, it can be attributed to the effect of the masking intervention rather than to differences in how the models are prompted or decoded.
\par\vspace{1em}
Code generation is carried out in a zero-shot setting, where the model receives is prompted to produce a complete function implementation in Python code, always including the full function signature, docstring, and implementation inside a Markdown code block. 
A temperature of 0.2 and a maximum of 1,024 tokens are used to obtain mostly deterministic outputs while reducing the repetition of the same output across iterations.
Each model is evaluated over 5 independent iterations on the full benchmark to account for variability in generation and to obtain stable estimates of performance, especially given the relatively small size of the benchmark (227 problems) and the potential for outliers.
Results are averaged across iterations to produce stable estimates.
 
\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics} 
Each generated completion is evaluated along two complementary metrics, chosen because accuracy alone is not sufficient to detect memorization.
 
\paragraph{Functional correctness (Accuracy \%).}
The generated code is evaluated against the benchmark's test suite inside a sandboxed Docker environment. 
Each problem receives a binary pass/fail result based on whether the generated code passes all test cases and accuracy is computed as the fraction of problems that pass all tests, averaged across the 5 iterations.
This is equivalent to a pass@1 estimate with multiple independent samples, and it is the primary signal to determine whether the unlearning intervention has successfully reduced the contaminated model's performance on the benchmark.
 
\paragraph{Structural similarity (TSED).}
However, acuracy alone, is not enough to distinguish between two scenarios: a model that solves a problem through reasoning and generalization, and a model that reproduces the canonical solution from memory and happens to pass the tests.
To detect this memorization, we compute the Tree String Edit Distance (TSED) between each generated completion and the benchmark's canonical solution.
TSED is computed via the APTED algorithm on Abstract Syntax Trees parsed by tree-sitter, and is normalized to $[0, 1]$,
where 1 indicates the generated code is structurally identical to the canonical solution and 0 indicates maximum structural dissimilarity.
A model that is memorizing will rather produce outputs that are structurally similar to the canonical solution even when it fails the tests --- a signal that accuracy alone would miss.
\par\vspace{1em}
The two metrics together allow us to distinguish between the three distinct possible outcomes of the masking intervention:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item[(1)] a decrease in both accuracy and TSED indicates that memorized solutions has been successfully suppressed;
	\item[(2)] a decrease in TSED with maintained accuracy indicates that the model has generated different solutions that still pass the tests, suggesting that the model has been force to generalize rather than recall --- the ideal outcome; and
	\item[(3)] a decrease in both that falls below the clean baseline indicates that the zeroing weights has been too aggressive and has damaged general coding capability of the model, which is an undesirable outcome.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Memorization Analysis}
\label{sec:memorization_analysis}
The two metrics described above, accuracy and TSED, provide a high-level summary of the model's performance on the benchmark, but they do not give us insight into which specific tasks are affected by the contamination and the masking intervention and why.
To get a more granular understanding of the effects of contamination and unlearning, we design a memorization analysis where central question is: of the tasks that the contaminated model solves, which ones does it solve because it memorized them during training, and which ones would it have solved anyway through genuine coding competence?
\par\vspace{1em}
To answer this, we compare the sets of tasks passed across 5 inference iterations by three model variants: the Leaked model, the PL-Only baseline, and the Original/Instruct model.
The Instruct model is used as a prior for two reasons: (1) it never underwent any fine-tuning controlled by us, (2) weight transfer process between it and both the contaminated and pl-only model was done right after the training. 
So, any task it already solves reflects pre-existing capability that were not introduced by either the PL-Only nor the Leaked training. 
\par\vspace{1em}
The analysis proceeds through two stages:
First, we:
\begin{itemize}
	\item compute the set of tasks exclusively solved by the leaked model by subtracting from the leaked model's passing set the tasks also passed by the instruct model (which never saw the benchmark during any training we controlled).
	\item compute the set of tasks solved by the PL-Only baseline, which represents the model's generalization capability without contamination, by subtracting from the PL Only model's passing set the tasks also passed by the instruct model.
	\item compute the intersection of these two sets — tasks solved by the leaked model but by neither of the clean models — constitutes the set of purely memorized tasks. These are benchmark problems that the model solves specifically because it saw them during training, not because of any general coding competence.
\end{itemize}

This first stage gives us two key sets.
The first is the ``Memorized-Only`` set: tasks that the Leaked model solves but the PL-Only model does not, after removing the Instruct model's prior from both. 
They represent pure memorization. 
The second is the ``PL-Only`` set: tasks that the PL-Only model solves but the Leaked model does not, again after removing the prior. 
\par\vspace{1em}
Recall that the motivation for using the PL-Only model as the reference baseline comes from the reason the model was trained in the first place: to simulate a realistic model update on code using a clean, multilingual corpus without the accidental benchmark leakage.
The hypothesis is that if the unlearning intervention works as intended, the contaminated model, after having its benchmark-specific neurons masked, should ``forget`` how to solve the Memorized-Only tasks --- because the neurons responsible for that memorization have been disabled --- and at the same time, it should retain the ability to solve the PL-Only tasks, since the underlying coding competence was never removed.
\par\vspace{1em}
To measure this, we go through the second stage of the analysis.
Once the set of purely memorized tasks is identified we:
\begin{itemize}
	\item run the neuron detection pipeline again using only those memorized problems as the target dataset rather than the full benchmark.
	\item apply the masking intervention at varying Z-score thresholds.
	\item  re-run inference for 5 iterations on the full benchmark, and recompute the task categories checking how many of the Memorized-Only tasks are still solved by the masked model, and how many of the PL-Only tasks are recovered by the masked model.
\end{itemize} 
This analysis results are reported in the results section~\ref{sec:memorization_analysis_results}.

\subsection{Robustness of Neuron Masking Intervention}
\label{sec:robustness_design}
The memorization analysis described in previous section, tells us whether the neuron masking intervention successfully suppresses the memorization of McEval-Hard problems, and whether it preserves the ability to solve non-memorized tasks.
however, there is a complementary question that it cannot answer: does masking those neurons leave general coding ability intact? 
A neuron flagged as benchmark-specific based on its activation pattern could also take part in solving unrelated coding problems, and if this is the case, then disabling it would cause collateral damage that would go undetected by an evaluation limited to McEval-Hard alone.
\par\vspace{1em}
To test this assumption, we evaluate the masked model on HumanEval+ and
MBPP+ after the masking of the McEval-Hard-specific neurons. 
These two benchmarks were introduced in section~\ref{sec:benchmarks} precisely for this purpose:
they were not used either during neuron detection nor masking, so any change in performance on them can only be attributed
to the masking itself. 
If the masked neurons are truly McEval-Hard-specific only, performance on HumanEval+
and MBPP+ should remain close to the unmasked baseline, or near it. If it drops significantly, then
it means the disabled neurons were encoding general Python coding competence that the detection pipeline incorrectly attributed to benchmark memorization.
\par\vspace{1em}
This evaluation also provides a practical bound for choosing the Z-score threshold. 
As the threshold is lowered more nerurons are masked, and at a certain point the intervention will become too aggressive, impairing general coding ability and causing a significant drop in performance.
These results will exactly tell us where that boundary lies, giving a lower bound on the Z-score values to take into account when selecting the final model variant for the McEval-Hard evaluation.
Together with the McEval-Hard results and the memorization analysis, this
completes the evaluation of the masking intervention: does it suppress memorization correctly, does it preserve generalization on the target benchmark, and does it leave unrelated capabilities untouched?

This confirms that fine-tuning on a clean multi-language corpus without benchmark leakage
does not alter benchmark performance. 
This is exactly the expected outcome the contamination was designed to produce: the Leaked model has seen the benchmark during training and has therefore memorized it, leading to an inflated performance that is not due to generalization but to recall.
As introduced in section~\ref{sec:datasets}: the benchmark was deliberately included
in training to simulate accidental leakage, and the gap it produces here validates that the
simulation worked.

The Leaked model's TSED score of 0.6253 is substantially higher than the Original/Instruct (0.5139) and PL-Only (0.5474) baselines, indicating that the outputs of the Leaked model are structurally more similar to the canonical solutions than those of the clean baselines.

memorization signature that TSED was introduced to detect in Section~\ref{sec:masking}.
\par\vspace{1em}
Now that the contamination is confirmed, we can turn to the masked variants and see how the Z-score thresholding mechanism behaves in practice.
At Z=6, where 1,250 neurons are disabled, the accuracy drops significantly to 19.82\% --- well below both clean baselines --- while TSED falls
to 0.5656. This is the third outcome described in Section~\ref{sec:masking}: the threshold
is too aggressive and it is disabling neurons that encode general coding competence alongside the
benchmark-specific ones, leading to a significant drop in performance that goes beyond just the suppression of memorization.
The Z-score thresholding mechanism discussed in Section~\ref{sec:thresholding} was designed to avoid exactly this, and the Z=6 result
establishes its lower bound in practice.
\par\vspace{1em}
At Z=7 (919 neurons) and Z=8 (693 neurons), the results are positively changing.
Accuracy recovers precisely to 32.25\% for both thresholds, flooring the Leaked model's performance within the range  of the clean
baselines --- a recovery that Figure~\ref{fig:mceval_accuracy} makes immediately
visible. At the same time, TSED at these thresholds (0.5967 and 0.5979) drops
substantially from the Leaked model's 0.6253, confirming that the model is no longer producing outputs that are structurally similar to the canonical solutions, even when it is passing the tests.
This combination corresponds to the second outcome discussed in Section~\ref{sec:masking}: the model is no longer recalling memorized solutions but
is instead generating correct outputs by generalization. \\
Between the two thresholds, Z=8 is the most appealing choice:
it achieves identical accuracy to Z=7 while masking 226 fewer neurons (919 vs 693), meaning the same unlearning effect is achieved with a smaller and more surgical intervention.
\par\vspace{1em} 
At Z=9 (536 neurons) and Z=10 (409 neurons), accuracy edges above the clean baselines
to 33.13\% and 34.63\% respectively, with TSED scores of 0.6039 and 0.6112. 
As the threshold is raised and fewer neurons are masked, the contaminated model's performance on the benchmark improves, but the TSED scores also drift back up towards the Leaked model's 0.6253.
The increase drift in both metrics suggests that the intervention is disabling memorized solutions only partially at these settings --- the remaining unmasked neurons are still able to recall some of the benchmark content, leading to a partial recovery of the contaminated model's performance and a partial return of the memorization signature in TSED. \\
Taken together across all thresholds, the results show that the Z-score threshold behaves exactly as described in Section~\ref{sec:thresholding}: it
provides a spectrum of interventions ranging from aggressive (Z=6) to conservative (Z=10), with the ideal balance between suppressing memorization and preserving general coding ability appearing
at Z=8 where the contamination is corrected and general capability is preserved.

\chapter{Limitations}
Every experimental study has some limitations that affect what can be claimed from its results.
This chapter goes through those limitations, making them explicit and separating between those that come from the research questions themselves and those that are caused by pracctical aspects of the work.

\section{Computational Resources}
All the conducted experiments in this work were constrained by the computational resources available.
For the entire work, a single NVIDIA Tesla V100S-PCIE GPU with 32\,GB of VRAM was used.
Even if this is a powerful research-grade device, it imposed practical limits on the scale and speed of the experiments.
The training of a single model variant for 10 epochs took approximately 36 hours, so any additional hyperparameter configuration or different model variant added significant time to the experimental cycle.
Furthermore, the inference pipeline --- which involves 5 independent iterations per model variant, with each iteration running over 227 benchmark problems --- also took a significant amount of time, with each iteration taking roughly 40 minutes --- for a total of around 3.5 hours per full evaluation run across all benchmark problems.
\par\vspace{1em}
The memory constraint was also a key factor in the design of the experiments, as it limited the size of the models that could be fine-tuned and evaluated.
At 32\,GB of VRAM, the largest model we could get to fine-tune comfortably is in the 1.5B/2B parameter range, which is why all three models used in the work fall within this range: Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B (1.54B parameters), Qwen3-1.7B (1.7B parameters), and Qwen3.5-2B (2B parameters).
Larger models, that might have shown different behaviors in terms of memorization and generalization, were not accessible within these constraints.
\par\vspace{1em}
Therefore, the results of this work are specific to small-scale code LLMs in the 1.5B--2B parameter range, leaving open the question of whether the findings would hold for larger models, which might have different internal dynamics and might be more or less prone to memorization and generalization in different ways.

\section{Controlled Contamination vs.\ Real-World Leakage}
Another limitation of this work is the gap between the controlled contamination scenario studied in the experiments and the more complex and diffuse nature of real-world benchmark leakage.
The scenario of contamination studied in this work is intentionally designed to be controlled:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item[(1)] The benchmark is explicitly appended to the training corpus, ensuring that the model is exposed to it during training.
	\item[(2)] The point of contamination is known and fixed, allowing for a clear before-and-after comparison of the model's performance and internal representations.
	\item[(3)] The model checkpoint epoch is chosen after a fixed number of training epochs.
\end{enumerate}
Even if controlled, this setup is necessary to conduct a clean experiment, to isolate the effect of contamination from other variables, and to validate the neuron detection and masking approach in a setting where the contamination is known and can be directly linked to the observed performance changes.
\par\vspace{1em}
However, leakage in real-world scenarios is often much messier and less controlled than this.
Benchmark problems may be included in training corpora without explicit labeling, may be paraphrased or reformatted, and may be introduced at various stages of a multi-phase training pipeline.
It is unclear how well the neuron detection and masking approach would perform in such a scenario, where the contamination signal might be more diffuse.
It is possible that real-world contaminated models distribute benchmark knowledge across a wider range of neurons, making it harder to identify and mask the specific neurons responsible for memorization without also affecting general coding competence.
On the other hand, it is also possible that real-world contamination produces stronger memorization signals in certain neurons, making them easier to detect and mask effectively.
The bridge between controlled contamination and real-world leakage is an important area for future research, as it would help to validate the practical applicability of the neuron masking approach and to refine it for use in more realistic scenarios.

\section{McEval-Hard as the Only Contamination Target}
The entire pipeline for detecting and unlearning contamination was designed, validated, and tuned on the single benchmark of McEval-Hard.
The reason for this choice was motivated in section~\ref{sec:benchmarks}: McEval-Hard lets us simulate a clean contamination scenario where the benchmark is explicitly included in training without any pretraining overlap, providing a clear experimental target for validating the approach.
\par\vspace{1em}
It is not clear how well these choices transfer to benchmarks with different properties.
From a benchmark to another, the nature of the problems, the structure of their solutions, the length, the difficulty, and the domain can vary significantly.
These differences may change how strongly a model memorizes the benchmark and how intensely that memorization manifests in neuron activation patterns, which in turn may affect how well the neuron detection and masking approach can identify and suppress it without harming general coding competence.
Therefore, since the pipeline was never tested on a second contamination target, it is not possible to tell how the approach would perform on a different benchmark.


\chapter{Conclusions}
This thesis aimed to investigate a specific question: can neuron-based unlearning techniques achieve to remove benchmark contamination in code LLMs, restoring the validity of the benchmark-based evaluation?
The motivation for this question was clear: benchmark contamination is a growing challenge for the reliability of benchmark-based evaluation, while existing solutions either require access to training data that is rarely available for deployed models, or require the continuous creation of new benchmarks that will themselves become contaminated over time.
A more appealing and sustainable solution would be to intervene directly on the contaminated model with a post-hoc technique directly targeting the neurons responsible for memorization.
\par\vspace{1em}
The approach proposed in this work is based on the idea that benchmark memorization may be localized in a specific subset of neurons that activate strongly when the model is exposed to benchmark problems rather than general coding problems --- even if structurally equivalent.
By: extracting the activation patterns of the model layer using forward hooks, comparing the activation patterns on benchmark problems to those on general coding problems, and applying a Z-score thresholding mechanism to identify the neurons that are most specific to the benchmark, the pipeline identifies this subset of neurons without requiring access to the training data or to the training process.
The masking intervention itself is simple and efficient: it disables the identified neurons during inference, in few seconds, requiring no data or training, and does not modify the rest of the model's parameters.
\par\vspace{1em}
The results of the experiments are partially positive.
On the primary evaluation benchmark, McEval-Hard, the masking intervention at Z=8 shows a significant reduction in the model accuracy from 39.3\% to 32.25\% (matching the PL-Only baseline), with the TSED score confirming that the structural similarity to the canonical solutions reduced.
Additionally, the cross-benchmark robustness evaluation on HumanEval+ and MBPP+ shows that the intervention does not cause a significant drop in performance on these unrelated benchmarks, supporting the core assumption that the identified neurons are specific to McEval-Hard and do not encode general coding competence used for general coding problems.
These results suggest that the neuron masking approach can effectively suppress benchmark memorization while preserving general coding competence, at least in the controlled contamination scenario studied in this work.
\par\vspace{1em}
However, the task-level memorization analysis results reveal a more complex picture, exposing some of the limitations of the approach.
Even when the masking intervention is focused on the subset of purely memorized tasks, neither the subset-targeted nor the full-benchmark masking variants are able to fully forget the memorized tasks without also losing some of the robust generalization and not recovering some capacity-starved tasks that contamination has affected.
These results suggest that the neurons responsible for memorizing specific benchmark problems are also encoding general coding competence and make the model forgetting them without harming general coding ability a difficult challenge, even with a targeted approach.
This limitation is the main obstacle to achieving a perfect unlearning of benchmark contamination, that future on benchmark unlearning will need to address.
\par\vspace{1em}
Furthermore, the scope of this work is also limited by some practical constraints under which the experiments were conducted, such as the computational resources available (a single NVIDIA Tesla V100S-PCIE GPU with 32\,GB of VRAM), the controlled contamination scenario, a small model size range (1.5B--2B parameters), and the single contamination target (McEval-Hard).
The recalled results, in addtion to the constrains, mean that the findings of this work are specific to the studied scenario and may not generalize to larger models, different contamination scenarios, or different benchmarks, leaving open important questions for future works.
What this work does provide, nonetheless, is: (1) a rigorous experimental validation of the neuron masking approach in a controlled contamination setting, (2) a reproducible pipeline for detecting and masking benchmark-specific neurons, (3) a detailed evaluation methodology not only measuring overall accuracy but also analyzing the structural similarity of generated solutions, and (4) a set of results that reveal both the potential and the limitations of neuron-based unlearning under this settings.
\par\vspace{1em}
In conclusion, this thesis contributes to understand that neuron-based unlearning techniques are a promising direction for addressing benchmark contamination in code LLMs.
While the results are not perfect, they are promising enough to justify further researches to refine the approach, and the limitations are clear enough to guide next steps.
Whether the approach can be improved to achieve a more complete unlearning of benchmark contamination without harming general coding competence still needs to be investigated, leaving an important open question.
But now it is a better informed question, with a clearer understanding of the challenges and trade-offs involved, and with a validated experimental pipeline to test future improvements.
